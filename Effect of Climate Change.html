Climate Change
EFFECT OF CLIMATE CHANGE

The effects of human-caused global warming are already occurring, are irreversible on the timeframe of individuals living today, and will intensify in the next decades.


Global climate change is already having an impact on the environment. Glaciers have reduced, ice on rivers and lakes is melting earlier, plant and animal ranges have altered, and trees are blooming earlier.


Scientists projected that global climate change will result in the following effects: loss of sea ice, rapid sea-level rise, and longer, more intense heat waves.


Scientists are confident that global temperatures will continue to rise for decades to come, owing in major part to greenhouse gases emitted by human activity. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), which includes over 1,300 scientists from the United States and other countries, predicts a 2.5 to 10 degree Fahrenheit temperature rise over the next century.


FUTURE EFFECTS:
  • Change Will Continue Through This Century and Beyond: The global climate is expected to alter more during the next century and beyond. The magnitude of climate change in the coming decades is mostly determined by the amount of heat-trapping gases generated globally and the sensitivity of the Earth's climate to those emissions
  • Temperature Will Continue To Rise: Because human-caused warming is being placed on a naturally changing climate, temperature rises have not been and will not be uniform or smooth across the country or over time.
  • Frost-free Season (and Growing Season) will Lengthen: Since the 1980s, the length of the frost-free season (and thus the corresponding growing season) has been increasing nationally, with the greatest increases occurring in the western United States, harming ecosystems and agriculture. The growing season is expected to prolong more over the United States. In the event that heat-trapping gas emissions continue to rise, the lengths of the frost-free and growing seasons are expected to extend by a month or more across most of the United States by the end of the century, with slightly smaller increases in the northern Great Plains. The western United States will experience the greatest increases in frost-free season (more than eight weeks), particularly in high elevation and coastal areas. If heat-trapping gas emissions are reduced, the increases will be much smaller.
  • Changes in Precipitation Patterns: Precipitation in the United States has grown on average since 1900, however certain places have had bigger increases than the national average, while others have seen losses. Over the next century, the northern United States will receive more winter and spring precipitation, while the Southwest will receive less. Future climate projections for the United States indicate that the existing trend of increased heavy precipitation occurrences will continue. Even in locations where overall precipitation is likely to decline, such as the Southwest, this pattern is expected to continue.
  • More Droughts and Heat Waves: Droughts in the Southwest and heat waves (periods of exceptionally hot weather lasting days to weeks) are expected to become more strong, while cold waves will become less intense. Summer temperatures are expected to rise further, and soil moisture will be reduced, exacerbating heat waves across much of the western and central United States. What were formerly once-in-a-century intense heat days (one-day events) are expected to occur every two or three years throughout much of the country by the end of this century.
  • Hurricanes Will Become Stronger and More Intense: Since the early 1980s, the intensity, frequency, and duration of North Atlantic storms, as well as the frequency of the strongest (Category 4 and 5) hurricanes, have all increased. The proportional contributions of natural and human factors to these increases are still unknown. Hurricane-related storm intensity and rainfall rates are expected to rise as the climate warms.
  • Sea Level Will Rise 1-8 feet by 2100: Since reliable record-keeping began in 1880, the global sea level has risen by around 8 inches. By 2100, it is expected to rise another 1 to 8 feet. This is due to the addition of water from melting land ice as well as the expansion of seawater as it warms. Storm surges and high tides, combined with sea-level rise and land subsidence, have the potential to exacerbate floods in many areas over the next several decades. Because the oceans take a long time to adjust to warmer temperatures at the Earth's surface, sea-level rise will continue past 2100. As a result, ocean waters will continue to warm and sea levels will rise at rates comparable to or greater than those observed in the current century for many centuries.
  • Arctic Likely to Become Ice-Free: The Arctic Ocean is predicted to be ice-free in the summer by mid-century.

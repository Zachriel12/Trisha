Climate change is defined as a change in a location's normal weather pattern. This might be due to a shift in the amount of rain that a location typically receives in a year. It might also represent a shift in a location's normal temperature for a month or season.

Climate change refers to changes in the Earth's climate. This might be due to a shift in the average temperature of the Earth. It might also represent a shift in where rain and snow normally fall on Earth.

The weather may change in a few hours. Climate change can take hundreds or even millions of years. There's also Earth's climate to consider. The Earth's climate is the result of combining all of the world's climates.

Climate change refers to a long-term shift in the average circumstances of a region, such as a temperature and rainfall. NASA scientists have discovered that the Earth's surface is warming, with many of the hottest years on record occurring in the last 20 years.

Global climate change refers to long-term average changes throughout the whole planet. These include rising temperatures and precipitation changes, as well as the impacts of global warmings, such as:

  • Increasing sea levels
  • Mountain glaciers are receding
  • Greenland, Antarctica, and the Arctic are melting at a quicker rate than typical.
  • Changes in the blooming seasons of flowers and plants.

Even before humans were on the scene, the Earth's climate was shifting. However, scientists have recently noticed unexpected alterations. For example, over the last 150 years, the average temperature of the Earth has risen significantly faster than expected.

WHAT IS CLIMATE CHANGE?

The climate of the Earth is always changing. There have been moments when the climate on Earth was warmer than it is now. There have been instances when the weather has been milder. These epochs can continue for thousands or even millions of years.

People who research the Earth's climate see that it is warming. In the previous 100 years, the Earth's temperature has risen by roughly one degree Fahrenheit. This may not appear to be much. Small variations in the Earth's temperature, on the other hand, can have large consequences.

Some impacts are already evident. Some snow and ice have melted as the Earth's temperature has warmed. Oceans have also risen as a result of global warming. It has also altered the time when certain plants develop.

HOW MUCH IS EARTH'S CLIMATE CHANGING RIGHT NOW?

Graph of change in annual global temperatures, compared to the average of global annual temperatures from 1880-1899. Credit: NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center

Some regions of the planet are warming faster than others. However, global air temperatures near the Earth's surface have risen by roughly 2 degrees Fahrenheit on average during the last century. Indeed, the last five years have been the hottest in millennia.

Many individuals, including scientists, are worried about global warming. The intensity and volume of rainfall during storms such as hurricanes are predicted to grow as the Earth's temperature continues to warm. Droughts and heatwaves will also grow more severe as the climate warms.

When the global temperature increases by one or two degrees, it can have a significant influence on the health of the planet's flora and animals.

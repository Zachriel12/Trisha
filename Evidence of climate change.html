EVIDENCE OF CLIMATE CHANGE
CLIMATE CHANGE: HOW MUCH DO WE KNOW?

This graph, based on the comparison of atmospheric samples contained in ice cores and more recent direct measurements, provides evidence that atmospheric CO2 has increased since the Industrial Revolution. (Credit: Luthi, D., et al.. 2008; Etheridge, D.M., et al. 2010; Vostok ice core data/J.R. Petit et al.; NOAA Mauna Loa CO2 record.)

Direct observations made on and above the Earth's surface reveal that the planet's climate is changing dramatically. Human activity is the major cause of these changes.

Earth’s climate has altered over time. There have been seven cycles of glacial advance and retreat in the last 650,000 years, with the abrupt end of the last ice age approximately 11,700 years ago marking the beginning of the current climatic era — and human civilization. The majority of these climatic shifts are linked to minute fluctuations in Earth's orbit that alter the quantity of solar energy our planet gets.

The current warming trend is particularly notable since it is undoubtedly the consequence of human activity since the mid-twentieth century and is occurring at an unprecedented rate spanning millennia. Human activities have undeniably warmed the atmosphere, ocean, and land, as well as caused extensive and fast changes in the atmosphere, ocean, cryosphere, and biosphere.

EVIDENCE FOR RAPID CHANGES:
1. GLOBAL TEMPERATURE RISE

Since the late 1800s, the average surface temperature of the Earth has risen by roughly 2.12 degrees Fahrenheit (1.18 degrees Celsius), owing primarily to rising carbon dioxide emissions into the atmosphere and other human activities. The majority of the warming has happened in the last 40 years, with the most recent seven years being the hottest. 2016 and 2020 are tied as the hottest years on record.

Summer sunshine and blue skies over the Nene valley in Northamptonshire. Credit: James Parker


2. WARMING OCEAN

The ocean has absorbed much of the extra heat, with the top 100 meters (328 feet) of water rising by more than 0.6 degrees Fahrenheit (0.33 degrees Celsius) since 1969. The oceans hold 90 percent of the excess energy on Earth.

Credit: U.S. Navy photo by Petty Officer 3rd Class Joshua Scott.


3. SHRINKING ICE SHEETS

The bulk of the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets has shrunk. Greenland lost an average of 279 billion tons of ice each year between 1993 and 2019, according to NASA's Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment data, while Antarctica lost around 148 billion tons of ice per year.


4. GLACIAL RETREATS

Glaciers are receding virtually everywhere on Earth, including the Alps, Himalayas, Andes, Rockies, Alaska, and Africa.

Picture of the Trient Glacier taken in 1891 is seen displayed in the same location in 2019

5. DECREASED SNOW COVER

Satellite data show that the quantity of spring snow cover in the Northern Hemisphere has reduced during the last five decades and that the snow is melting sooner.

Northern hemisphere snow cover (white) and sea ice (yellow) 10 October 2020 (left) and 2021 (right). Map source: National Ice Center (NIC).


6. SEA LEVEL RISE

Over the previous century, the global sea-level rise by roughly 8 inches (20 cm). The rate in the recent two decades, on the other hand, is over double that of the previous century and is increasing somewhat each year.


7. DECLINING ARCTICE SEA ICE

Over the last several decades, both the area and thickness of Arctic sea ice have decreased dramatically.


8. EXTREME EVENTS

Since 1950, the number of record hot temperature events in the United States has increased while the number of record low-temperature events has decreased. The United States has also seen an increase in the number of severe rainfall events

David McNew/Getty Images News/Getty Images


9. OCEAN ACIDIFICATIONS

According to NOOA, the acidity of surface ocean waters has grown by roughly 30% since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution. This rise is due to people emitting more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, which is then absorbed by the ocean. In recent decades, the ocean has absorbed between 20% and 30% of total human carbon dioxide emissions ( 7.2 to 10.8 billion metric tons per year).

In summer 2015, the Australian Antarctic Division carried out the first free ocean carbon enrichment experiment in the Antarctic under permanent ice near Casey Base. CO2-enriched seawater with varying levels of dissolved CO2 is pumped from the surface into plexiglas chambers positioned on the seafloor to measure the impact on the seafloor flora and fauna.
